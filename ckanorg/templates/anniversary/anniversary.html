{{ _("Our Story") }}
{{ _("From a frustrated student to global infrastructure") }}

{{ _("In the early 2000s,") }} Rufus Pollock {{ _("was trying to answer big questions — how many people can the Earth support? The data was either impossible to find or locked behind paywalls costing thousands of dollars.") }}

{{ _("Inspired by CPAN — the Perl package archive — he built CKAN: a catalog for data. Two decades later, it powers national open data portals on every continent, from data.gov to open.canada.ca to datos.gob.mx.") }}

{{ _("CKAN is now recognized as a") }} {{ _("Digital Public Good") }}, {{ _("helping achieve 9 of the 17 UN Sustainable Development Goals.") }}

{{ _("You'd read a book with loads of data... but when you went looking for the datasets, they just weren't available. The data existed — it just wasn't findable. That was the problem CKAN was built to solve.") }}
— {{ _("Rufus Pollock, Founder of CKAN") }}

2006
{{ _("CKAN v0.1: the first commit") }}{{ _("Milestone") }}▼
Rufus Pollock releases the first version of CKAN under the Open Knowledge Foundation. Inspired by CPAN, it was a simple catalog for open datasets.
"CKAN was never meant to become a big open source project. It started because I needed something that didn't exist — a CPAN for data."— Rufus Pollock, Founder
2010
{{ _("CKAN 1.0 & data.gov.uk launch") }}{{ _("Milestone") }}▼
Version 1.0 introduced critical improvements to authorization, search capabilities, and resource previews — and saw a major new production deployment with CKAN powering data.gov.uk.
2011
{{ _("CKAN 1.3: harvesting & federation") }}{{ _("Release") }}▼
CKAN 1.3 introduced harvesting capabilities, enabling users to aggregate and import data from external sources. A pivotal step toward federated open data ecosystems.
2013
{{ _("CKAN 2.0 & Data.gov migration") }}{{ _("Milestone") }}▼
Version 2.0 brought a significant architectural overhaul that drastically improved performance and scalability, supporting multiple organizations and enhancing user management and permissions. That same year, the US Government migrated Data.gov to CKAN, and Canada launched data.gc.ca (now open.canada.ca), featuring a comprehensive and fully bilingual metadata schema.
2015
{{ _("Linked data, previews & visualizations") }}{{ _("Release") }}▼
CKAN 2.3 brought support for linked data and the semantic web. CKAN 2.4 introduced resource views for tabular data, enabling users to visualize and explore their data in new ways.
2019
{{ _("Datopian & Link Digital become co-stewards") }}{{ _("Community") }}▼
The Open Knowledge Foundation Board supported the joint stewardship proposal put forward by Link Digital and Datopian, with OKF remaining as purpose trustee to ensure the stewards remain true to the purpose and ethos of the CKAN project.
2020
{{ _("CKAN 2.9: built for the modern web") }}{{ _("Release") }}▼
CKAN 2.9 replaced CKAN's web framework from Pylons to Flask and added support for Python 3. A landmark release that future-proofed the platform for years to come.
2021
{{ _("CKAN Monthly Live launches") }}{{ _("Community") }}▼
Launched in June 2021, CKAN Monthly Live brought the global community together every third Wednesday for open demos, discussions, and connection — organised and moderated by Yoana Popova.
2023
{{ _("Recognized as a Digital Public Good") }}{{ _("Milestone") }}▼
CKAN was recognized as a Digital Public Good for addressing multiple United Nations Sustainable Development Goals through enhanced data accessibility.
2024
{{ _("CKAN 2.11: performance & data quality") }}{{ _("Release") }}▼
CKAN 2.11 brought performance improvements in template and snippet rendering, as well as the new Table Designer feature to easily create and validate DataStore resources from the UI.
2026
{{ _("20th Anniversary: the celebration year") }}{{ _("Milestone") }}▼
CKAN 2.12 — the final release in the 2.x series — lays the architectural groundwork for CKAN 3.0. The global community gathers to celebrate two decades of open source, open data, and open collaboration. You're part of this moment.
